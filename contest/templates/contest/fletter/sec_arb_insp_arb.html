Step 1: Apply for dispute resolution

So your landlord is trying to keep your deposit despite not properly inspecting your unit. Your landlord has your forwarding address and you have written the landlord a letter requesting the return of your deposit. You are going to need some evidence and energy to get your deposit back.

Your next step is to apply for dispute resolution through B.C's Residential Tenancy Branch.

You can apply for dispute resolution:

  • online through the Residential Tenancy Branch website, or
  • in person at an RTB Office.

    •

Why go through dispute resolution?

It is important to not let your landlord rip you off. If successful you can get the Residential Tenancy Branch to:

  • Order your landlord to return twice the amount of the deposit, and
  • Recover the cost of the filling fees
      -see Clicklaw B.C's page on getting your deposit back for more info.

Fee Waiver?

Dispute resolution costs $100. However, this fee may be waived if you submit a Fee Waiver Application along with proof that you have a low income.

You can also get your fee back if you are successful

Step 2: Serve your landlord

Now that you have applied you have 3 calendar days to give your landlord notice of dispute resolution and hearing documents.

There are two ways you can 'serve' these documents to your landlord.

  • In person: Bring a witness with you.
  • Send by registered mail: Keep the receipt from the post office to prove you sent the notice. Mail the documents to the landlord as soon as you receive them.

Step 3: Prepare Evidence

You will need to present evidence at your hearing. Examples of evidence you should consider providing include:

If you didn't send evidence with your application you are required to serve copies of your evidence to both the Residential Tenancy Branch (RTB) and the other party 14 days before the hearing.

How to Prepare Your Evidence

The first page of your evidence package should be an index clearly stating a list of all your evidence along with page numbers.

Any witnesses should be present at the hearing. If they can't come in person you'll need to get a sworn statement from a lawyer or notary.

Digital Evidence

There are special rules for digital evidence. You'll need to ensure that both the RTB and your landlord can play the digital evidence during the hearing.

You'll need to ask both the landlord and the RTB to tell you if they have the equipment available to play your evidence.

You must also submit the RTB's Digital Evidence Details Form.
