Step 1: Apply for dispute resolution

So, you've tried to get your landlord to make repairs, even given them a deadline, and now the deadline has passed.

Your next step is to apply for dispute resolution through B.C's Residential Tenancy Branch.

You can apply for dispute resolution:

  • online through the Residential Tenancy Branch website, or
  • in person at an RTB Office.

    •

Why go through dispute resolution?

If successful you can get the Residential Tenancy Branch to:

  • order your landlord to fix the problem,
  • order your landlord to lower your rent until the problem is fixed,
  • give you permission to pay for the repairs yourself and to deduct the cost of the repair from your rent, or
  • order you to pay your rent to the Residential Tenancy Branch instead of to your landlord, until the repairs are done; this is called a "re-direction of rent."
  • compensate you for your time spent without something essential like a stove, fridge, or toilet
      -see The Tenant's Survival Guide for more info.

Fee Waiver?

Dispute resolution costs $100. However, this fee may be waived if you submit a Fee Waiver Application along with proof that you have a low income.

Step 2: Serve your landlord

Now that you have applied you have 3 calendar days to give your landlord notice of dispute resolution and hearing documents.

There are two ways you can 'serve' these documents to your landlord.

  • In person: Bring a witness with you.
  • Send by registered mail: Keep the receipt from the post office to prove you sent the notice. Mail the documents to the landlord as soon as you receive them.

Step 3: Prepare Evidence

You will need to present evidence at your hearing. Examples of evidence you should consider providing include:

  • Pictures
  • Copies of written communication
  • Your tenancy agreement
  • Video/audio recordings
  • Witness statements
  • Cost estimates from a tradesman
  • Receipts

If you didn't send evidence with your application you are required to send copies of your evidence to both the Residential Tenancy Branch (RTB) and the other party 14 days before the hearing.

How to Prepare Your Evidence

The first page of your evidence package should be an index clearing stating a list of all your evidence along with page numbers.

Your witnesses should come to the hearing. If they can't come in person you'll need to get an expensive sworn statement from a lawyer or notary.

Digital Evidence

Digital Evidence

For some reason there are special rules for digital evidence. You'll need to ensure that both the RTB and your landlord can play the digital evidence during the hearing.

You'll need to ask both the landlord and the RTB to tell you if they have the equipment available to play your evidence.

You must also submit the RTB's Digital Evidence Details Form.

Call a Building inspector

Sometimes you need powerful evidence to make your case. One option is to call the City to get your building inspected.

The City of Vancouver's Standards of Maintenance bylaw contains basic housing standards your landlord has to comply with.

Call a city inspector by dialing 311. Careful though, if you're living in an illegal suite this tactic could seriously backfire
